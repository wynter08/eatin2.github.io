Department Details Grouped by School of Learning

School of Medicine

Surgery

The Department of Surgery operates across three core pillars: clinical care, medical education, and biomedical research. Clinical care spans a wide range of subcategories and surgical scenarios, from trauma and critical care to oncology and transplant procedures. The educational mission focuses on training students to become experienced surgeons through structured residency programs at affiliated hospitals. Surgeon scientists also contribute to groundbreaking biomedical research, developing new techniques and technologies that shape the future of operative medicine.

Courses

  • Gross Anatomy, Surgical Pathology, Systemic Physiology
  • Core Surgery Clerkship (8–12 week clinical rotation)
  • Surgical Critical Care & Trauma
  • Internships and Electives

Skills

  • Procedural Proficiency (knot tying, suturing, instrumentation)
  • Patient Management and Complication Handling
  • Cognitive and Interpersonal Communication
  • Procedural Simulations

Learning Outcomes

  • Excellence in Patient Care
  • Comprehensive Medical Knowledge
  • Professionalism and Systems-Based Practice

Specializations

  • Subspecialty focus on specific organ systems
  • Goal-oriented tracks (e.g., plastic surgery, neurosurgery)
  • Robotic and minimally invasive surgical techniques

Biochemistry & Molecular Genetics

This department emphasizes research-driven inquiry and the advanced use of technology. Faculty and students investigate cancer biology, gene regulation, computational biology, structural biology, and drug design. The curriculum integrates foundational chemical and biological sciences with specialized molecular study, preparing graduates for careers in academic research, pharmaceutical development, and translational medicine.

Courses

  • General & Organic Chemistry, Cell Biology, General Genetics
  • Macromolecular Structure, Molecular Biology
  • Cellular Metabolism, Advanced Molecular Genetics
  • Molecular Biology of Cancer, Enzymology, Bioinformatics
  • Receptors & Signal Transduction, Pharmacology

Skills

  • Laboratory Techniques and Protocols
  • Research Design and Execution
  • Communication and Collaboration
  • Data Analysis and Interpretation

Learning Outcomes

  • Formulate Testable Scientific Hypotheses
  • Interpret and Analyze Complex Data Sets
  • Conduct Independent Experiments
  • Understand Ethical Standards in Research

Specializations

  • Cancer Biology and Gene Regulation
  • Computational Biology and Structural Biology
  • Drug Design and Development

School of Engineering & Applied Sciences

Biomedical Engineering

Biomedical Engineering merges traditional engineering principles of physics and mathematics with biological sciences and medicine, using technology and engineering methodologies to address medical challenges. The discipline incorporates technology at every level of cellular organization and develops a wide range of medical instruments and devices. This interdisciplinary approach prepares students for careers in both engineering and medical fields, bridging the gap between physical sciences and human health.

Courses

  • Calculus I–III, Differential Equations, Linear Algebra
  • General & Organic Chemistry, Physics
  • Engineering Statics, Dynamics, Circuit Analysis
  • Thermodynamics, Computer Programming
  • Biomechanics, Biomaterials, Bioinstrumentation
  • Physiology for Engineers, Capstone Design

Skills

  • Technical Engineering Proficiency
  • Analytical and Quantitative Thinking
  • Communication and Teamwork

Learning Outcomes

  • Apply Engineering, Biology, and Math Principles to Bio Problems
  • Analyze Complex Biological and Engineering Data
  • Design Solutions Within Defined Constraints

Specializations

  • Biomechanics
  • Tissue Engineering
  • Medical Imaging
  • Bioinstrumentation and Sensors
  • Computational Biology and Bioinformatics

Electrical & Computer Engineering

Electrical and Computer Engineering encompasses the design and development of electrical systems, circuits, and computing hardware. The field has increasingly integrated artificial intelligence, data science, and sustainable technology into its curriculum and research. With broad applications spanning semiconductors, telecommunications, robotics, and power systems, this department offers one of the most versatile career pipelines in engineering.

Courses

  • Circuits and Electronics
  • Signals and Systems
  • Digital Logic Systems
  • Microprocessors
  • Electromagnetics

Skills

  • Programming and Software Development
  • Design and Simulation
  • Systems Engineering
  • Data Analysis

Learning Outcomes

  • Problem Formulation and Resolution
  • System-Level Design and Integration
  • Experimental Methodology
  • Collaborative Teamwork

Specializations

  • Computer Systems and Hardware Design
  • Communications and Networking
  • Power and Energy Systems
  • Control Systems and Robotics
  • Electromagnetics and Applied Physics

School of Law

Corporate & Business Law

Corporate and Business Law examines the legal rules governing commercial activity. Corporate Law and Governance addresses the comprehensive rules regarding nearly every aspect of companies, including formation, taxation, mergers and acquisitions, securities regulation, raising capital, and antitrust and competition law. Students develop skills in contract drafting, legal analysis, risk management, and negotiation.

Courses

  • Business Associations & Corporations
  • Contracts and Sales
  • Securities Regulation
  • Corporate Governance and Finance
  • Labor and Income Law
  • Regulatory Compliance

Skills

  • Contract Drafting and Review
  • Legal Analysis and Reasoning
  • Risk Management
  • Negotiation and Communication

Learning Outcomes

  • Sound Legal and Ethical Reasoning
  • Professional Responsibility
  • Readiness for Practice
  • Business-Strategy Alignment

Specializations

  • Corporate Law and Governance
  • Securities and Finance
  • Commercial Law and Bankruptcy
  • Tax Law, Technology Law
  • Employment and Labor Law
  • Antitrust and Competition

Family & Domestic Law

Family and Domestic Law addresses the legal rules, social policies, and societal frameworks governing intimate relationships and the family unit. Key topics include marriage and divorce, parent-child relationships, domestic violence, and how various family dynamics shape and are shaped by legislation. The field blends insights from sociology, psychology, economics, and social work to serve families and communities.

Courses

  • Marriage, Divorce, and Family Formation
  • Child Support, Custody, and Juvenile Law
  • Domestic Violence and the Law
  • Adoption and Reproductive Technology
  • Estate and Tax Planning in Divorce
  • Firsthand Clinical Experience

Skills

  • Interviewing and Counseling
  • Negotiation and Mediation
  • Legal Analysis
  • Document Drafting and Trial Practice

Learning Outcomes

  • Legal Comprehension in Family Contexts
  • Creative Problem Solving
  • Professionalism and Empathy

Specializations

  • Matrimonial and Divorce Law
  • Child and Juvenile Law
  • Elder Law
  • Domestic Violence
  • Reproductive Technology
  • International and Comparative Family Law

Geographic Trends & Observations

Urban Clustering

  • Medicine, engineering, and law programs cluster heavily in major metropolitan areas
  • These hubs attract funding, faculty, and industry partnerships
  • Rural and remote regions face significant access gaps

Industry Shaping

  • Local economies directly shape academic strengths (e.g., Silicon Valley for ECE, financial hubs for corporate law)
  • Regional industries drive curriculum relevance and internship pipelines

State-Level Variation

  • State laws influence family law curriculum and practice across regions
  • North American agglomeration creates specialized research networks but also geographic disparities in resources and training quality

Reflection

Personal Insights on Academic Exploration

Based on the research conducted, the academic departments that interest me the most are Biochemistry and Molecular Genetics as well as Biomedical Engineering. These two fields align closely with my current interests in science, particularly biological areas, as well as the innovation, design, and research involved in developing new technologies. I believe these subjects blend multiple academic disciplines and serve as a bridge between the physical sciences, engineering, and medicine.

Something that thoroughly surprised me was the extent of interdisciplinary connection and cooperation across these fields. As modern education becomes less focused on individual topics and departments, technological innovation allows for greater incorporation of diverse ideas and knowledge to advance each discipline. Another aspect I was previously unaware of was the organizational structure behind how universities are arranged. This includes the subdivisions of colleges, schools, departments, and majors, as well as the various types of degrees and steps, such as research and internships, that are needed to progress academically in higher education.

Academic departments and fields evolve constantly through multiple factors. Through this research, the most common drivers proved to be funding, new technology, breakthroughs in information, and changing worldviews both socially and economically. A key pattern across these fields is the prevalence of further specialization and targeted tracks within departments. This is driven by new technologies, such as robotics in medicine or the introduction of artificial intelligence in data sciences and engineering. Another factor is a shift in worldviews, as more women enter higher education and fill roles historically held by men, and globalization allows more people to have access. These changes lead to greater innovation beyond any single field and foster deeper integration between academic schools and departments.

This research has brought to my attention that simply following the standard public school curriculum is not sufficient to provide a thorough understanding of the academic structure of higher education. However, I also recognize that this research alone is not exhaustive, as I have only begun to explore this system and the information behind it. I believe the necessary next steps include further research to gain a broader understanding, attending programs to experience them firsthand, and pursuing personal projects to explore different academic departments in greater depth.
